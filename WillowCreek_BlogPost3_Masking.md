# What Is Masking in Autism and How Does It Affect Your Mental Health?

Have you ever left a social event and felt completely hollowed out — not just tired, but like you'd been performing for hours and the curtain had finally come down? Have you spent so much energy monitoring how you speak, move, and react that by the time you get home, you can barely function?

If you're Autistic, there's a name for what you've been doing: masking. And it might be costing you more than you realize.

---

## What Is Masking?

Masking — sometimes called "camouflaging" — is when an Autistic person hides or suppresses their natural traits in order to appear more neurotypical. It's a way of fitting in, avoiding judgment, and staying safe in a world that wasn't built for the way your brain works.

Masking can look like:

- Forcing eye contact even when it's uncomfortable
- Scripting conversations in your head before (and during) them
- Suppressing stimming (rocking, flapping, fidgeting) in public
- Mimicking the facial expressions, tone, and body language of the people around you
- Laughing at jokes you don't find funny or pretending to understand social cues that genuinely don't make sense to you
- Rehearsing how to act "normal" in situations you find overwhelming

For many Autistic people, masking has been happening for so long it feels automatic — like a second skin. Some people don't even realize they're doing it until they start learning about autism later in life.

---

## Why Do Autistic People Mask?

Masking isn't a choice so much as a survival strategy. Most Autistic people learn early — often through painful experience — that their natural way of being isn't always welcomed. They get told they're "too much," "too intense," "weird," or "difficult." They're excluded, misunderstood, or corrected.

So they adapt. They study other people like a second language. They learn to perform "normal" to avoid the consequences of being visibly different.

For women and girls especially, this is compounded by socialization. Girls are often expected to be socially adept, emotionally attuned, and accommodating — which means many Autistic women become exceptionally skilled at masking and go undiagnosed for years (or decades) as a result. Their autism gets missed because they've learned to hide it so well.

> *"I spent so many years performing 'okay' that I forgot what it felt like to actually be okay."*

---

## The Mental Health Cost of Masking

Masking is exhausting. Not "I need a nap" exhausting — genuinely depleting in a way that accumulates over time and can seriously affect mental health.

**Anxiety and depression** are extremely common in Autistic people who mask heavily. When you spend all day suppressing who you are and monitoring every word and gesture, your nervous system stays in a near-constant state of high alert. That wears on you.

**Autistic burnout** — a state of deep physical, emotional, and cognitive exhaustion — is often the result of prolonged masking. It can look like depression, but it's distinct: it usually involves a temporary or lasting loss of skills and functioning, particularly in areas like communication and executive function. Recovery can take months.

**Identity confusion** is another significant impact. If you've been masking since childhood, you may genuinely struggle to know who you are without the mask. What do you actually enjoy? How do you actually want to communicate? What are your real needs? These questions can feel disorienting and even grief-inducing.

**Burnout from misdiagnosis** is also common — because masking is so effective, many Autistic people get diagnosed with anxiety, depression, or borderline personality disorder before (or instead of) autism, and spend years in treatment that doesn't fully address what's actually happening.

## What Does Unmasking Look Like?

Unmasking isn't about flipping a switch. It's a slow, often non-linear process of learning which parts of yourself you've been hiding — and deciding, in safe contexts, to let them be visible.

This might mean:
- Stimming when you need to, without shame
- Being honest about what social situations are genuinely difficult for you
- Asking for accommodations at work or in relationships
- Letting yourself be quiet, or intense, or literal, without immediately apologizing for it
- Spending more time with people who don't require you to perform

Unmasking works best when it happens in safe environments — with people who accept you, and at a pace that feels manageable. Dropping the mask all at once in every context isn't necessarily the goal, and for many people it's not realistic or even safe. The goal is to have *some* spaces where you don't have to perform. And to let those spaces grow over time.

---

## Therapy Can Help — If It's the Right Kind

Not all therapy is helpful for Autistic people who mask. Approaches that focus on making you appear more neurotypical — better eye contact, more "appropriate" social responses — are essentially teaching you to mask better. That's not healing.

Neurodiversity-affirming therapy takes a different approach. It starts from the premise that there is nothing wrong with being Autistic. The goal isn't to make you fit the neurotypical mould — it's to help you understand your own nervous system, identify what you actually need, process the grief and exhaustion that often come with unmasking, and build a life that works for your brain.

For many people, this also involves working through trauma. Years of being misunderstood, corrected, and excluded leave marks. A good therapist can help you untangle those experiences and begin to rebuild a sense of self that isn't built around hiding.

---

## You Were Never Broken

Masking develops because the world often makes it necessary. But the need to mask says something about the world — not about you.

Your natural way of communicating, processing, and experiencing the world isn't a flaw to be corrected. It's a different way of being human. And you deserve spaces — and support — that honour that.

If you're starting to recognize masking in your own life, or if you're exhausted in a way that goes beyond ordinary tired, you don't have to keep navigating it alone.
